Predicting the Future of Water Stress¶

By: Spencer Potter

The Importance of Water Stress and Availability¶

Water is a limited resource that all humans need to survive. Furthermore countries are reliant on water to sustain agriculture, help generate energy, and to aid in the creation of many things in industry. The main withdrawal of fresh water is for agriculture at 70 percent, according to the world bank (https://www.worldbank.org/en/topic/water-in-agriculture). As these resources dwindle, conflicts will arise around the world. This can be seen all over the world, from California where farmers are in conflict with Los Angeles, and over the Euphrates and Tigres river where Iraq, Turkey, and Syria are in tension over the use of the rivers.

The purpose of this project is to look at data about water stress in the Americas, explore what variables seem to have an impact on water stress, visualize these connections, and try to predict water stress in the future. This would allow regions to come to agreements and rationalizations before the water stress is on the country.

Data Exploration¶

The data has four variables

  1. water stress, this is a metric that shows how much stress a region is under. This is a measure of how much water is being used as opposed to the available water
  2. dependency ratio, this is a percentage that shows how dependent on water imports a country is
  3. total renewable water per capita, this is a measure of how much renewable water a country has per person
  4. freshwater withdrawal as percent of total renewable water resources, this shows how much water is being pulled in relation to total resources

This data was pulled from AQUASTAT and includes some metrics that are not important and does not include some that would be helpful for graphing. To look at only region we will need to add a new column in pre-processing.

# This for loops is going through the dataframe grabbing the dependency ratio, fresh water, water stress, and total renewable water values for the given country and year
# Then it sets the new columns as these values
for idx, row in data.iterrows():
  dep_ratio = data[(data['Variable'] == 'Dependency ratio') & (data['Country'] == row.Country) & (data['Year'] == row.Year)].values
  fresh_w = data[(data['Variable'] == 'MDG 7.5. Freshwater withdrawal as % of total renewable water resources') & (data['Country'] == row.Country) & (data['Year'] == row.Year)].values
  wat_str = data[(data['Variable'] == 'SDG 6.4.2. Water Stress') & (data['Country'] == row.Country) & (data['Year'] == row.Year)].values
  renew = data[(data['Variable'] == 'Total renewable water resources per capita') & (data['Country'] == row.Country) & (data['Year'] == row.Year)].values


  data.at[idx, 'Dependency_Ratio'] = dep_ratio[0][4]
  data.at[idx, 'Fresh_Water'] = fresh_w[0][4]
  data.at[idx, 'Water_Stress'] = wat_str[0][4]
  data.at[idx, 'Total_renewable'] = renew[0][4]

Conclusion¶

After going through the process of gathering data, processing the data, visualizing the data, and analyzing the data it is seen that water stress mainly depends on the amount of freshwater withdrawal a country is doing. It also depends on the region, which makes sense as the environment that you are in would be a strong indicator of water availability. The model that was built had an accuracy of around 88 percent within one percent of the true water stress.
